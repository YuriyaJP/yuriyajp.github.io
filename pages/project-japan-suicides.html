The Problem

A Tokyo-based mental health organization provides crisis intervention services and prevention programs aimed at reducing psychological distress and supporting at-risk individuals. As the organization seeks to engage more extensively with grant funders and evaluators, there is an opportunity to complement existing activity-based reporting (such as service utilization and program delivery) with additional outcome-oriented measures that many funders increasingly request. These measures may include estimates of lives impacted, health outcomes expressed in Disability-Adjusted Life Years (DALYs), and cost-effectiveness indicators.

My Approach

I designed a comprehensive cost-effectiveness analysis methodology that integrated:

  • Epidemiological baseline: Suicide rates and at-risk population data
  • Intervention reach: 97+ prevention workshops and 9,000+ crisis contacts annually, for the purpose of this evaluation rated as 15% intervention
  • Cost modeling: Lives saved costs per intervention and cost-per-DALY metrics

Key Insights

  • Working-age groups (20–59) dominate economic loss
  • Annual productivity loss exceeds ¥300B
  • Targeted interventions show strong ROI potential

DALY Calculation Methodology

The DALY Formula

Disability-Adjusted Life Years (DALYs) measure the burden of disease by combining years of life lost (YLL) due to premature death and years lived with disability (YLD).

DALY = YLL + YLD

YLL = N × L
N = number of deaths
L = standard life expectancy at age of death

My Implementation for Suicide Prevention

For this mental health intervention analysis, I focused primarily on Years of Life Lost (YLL) from prevented suicides, as the intervention targets crisis prevention rather than chronic disability management.

Key Assumptions Used

  • Life expectancy: Japan standard life tables by age (80+ years for youth)
  • Intervention effectiveness: 15% reduction in suicide incidence based on peer-reviewed meta-analyses of crisis intervention programs
  • At-risk population: Youth contacts through workshops (97 events) and crisis hotline (9,000+ contacts)
  • Baseline risk: National youth suicide rates from Japan's Ministry of Health, Labour and Welfare
  • Attribution model: Conservative estimate accounting for multiple protective factors beyond single intervention

Calculation Steps

  1. Identify at-risk population: Youth reached through prevention programs (workshops + hotline contacts)
  2. Apply baseline risk: Expected deaths without intervention = Population × Baseline suicide rate
  3. Calculate prevented deaths: Baseline deaths × Intervention effectiveness (15%)
  4. Compute YLL: For each prevented death, YLL = Life expectancy at age of death
  5. Sum total DALYs averted: Aggregate across all age groups and intervention types
  6. Calculate cost-effectiveness: Total program cost ÷ DALYs averted = Cost per DALY

Example Calculation

At-risk youth reached: 9,097 contacts
Baseline suicide rate (15-24): 13.7 per 100,000
Expected deaths without intervention: 9,097 × (13.7/100,000) = 1.25 deaths
Prevented deaths (15% reduction): 1.25 × 0.15 = 0.19 deaths
YLL per prevented death (age 20): 80 - 20 = 60 years
Total DALYs averted: 0.19 × 60 = 11.4 DALYs
Program cost: ¥25,000,000
Cost per DALY: ¥2,193,000 (~$14,600)

Note: This is a simplified example. The full analysis stratified by age groups, intervention types (workshops vs. hotline), and incorporated confidence intervals around effectiveness estimates.
